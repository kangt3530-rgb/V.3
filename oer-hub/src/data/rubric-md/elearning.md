

**Open 4 Peer Review Project**  
**eLearning Rubric (Single-Point Rubric)**

The *Open 4 Peer Review* Project aims to increase faculty adoption and adaptation of Open Educational Resources (OER) by developing and applying standards and rubrics to course materials for high-enrollment courses. A barrier to faculty use of OER is the lack of qualitative indicators that can support robust peer review. A consortium of colleges and universities worked to address this barrier by (1) adapting existing, and creating new, openly licensed standards of quality for OER, (2) developing corresponding rubrics for applying those standards in a peer review process.

**Operational Definition for Accessibility**

***eLearning*** for Open Educational Resources (OER) refers to the degree to which digital tools, technologies and/or platforms integrated into educational materials demonstrate functional excellence, pedagogical effectiveness, technical reliability, and accessibility for diverse learners. High-quality eLearning seamlessly support learning objectives through intuitive interfaces, robust functionality, cross-platform compatibility, and evidence of enhancing student engagement and learning outcomes. Quality eLearning tools in OER maintain data privacy and security standards, provide adequate technical support, facilitate both instructor presence and student collaboration, and can be effectively scaled and customized to meet varied educational contexts and learner needs.

**Framing Language**

***Single-point rubric format.*** A single-point rubric differs from traditional holistic or analytic rubrics by focusing on a single column that describes the expected standard or proficient performance for each criterion. Rather than providing multiple performance levels with detailed descriptions, this format presents the target expectation in the center column, with space for feedback on areas needing improvement (left column) and areas exceeding expectations (right column). This design promotes clearer communication of standards while allowing for personalized, constructive feedback that guides improvement and recognizes excellence.

***Singularly focused rubric.*** Each of the rubrics in the Open 4 Peer Review suite of rubrics is designed to focus on one six areas for OER peer review: (1) Accessibility, (2) Copy Editing, (3) Copyright, (4) Disciplinary Appropriateness, (5) eLearning, and (6) Universal Design for Learning. **This rubric focuses exclusively on eLearning, and reviewers should therefore focus only on evaluating the OER on the criteria and the associated established standards of quality articulated on this rubric.** Review of the same OER with the other Open 4 Peer Review rubrics provide the most appropriate way to address issues/concerns unrelated to eLearning.

***Intended use*** This rubric is designed to support faculty peer review of open educational resources to ensure standards of quality are met before adoption or publication. Specifically, this rubric helps reviewers evaluate whether digital tools and technologies embedded within or accompanying OER materials demonstrate the functionality, pedagogical effectiveness, technical reliability, and accessibility expected for effective online and technology-enhanced learning. This rubric is discipline-agnostic and can be applied to OER materials from any field that incorporate digital learning tools. Faculty/educators can edit the rubric's existing criteria and/or add additional criteria if they deem it necessary for their specific context.

***Nature of the data generated by the rubric.*** Data generated by this rubric are ***categorical*** in nature. Categorical data can be sorted into distinct groups or categories rather than measured on a continuous numerical scale. In rubric scoring, categorical data refers to performance levels (e.g., "Does Not Meet," "Meets," "Exceeds") that represent qualitatively different categories of achievement rather than precise numerical measurements. Each category describes a distinct level of quality, and scores represent classification into these discrete groups rather than points on a continuous scale. This approach emphasizes descriptive feedback and developmental progression rather than numerical ranking. 

***Threshold for achieving “Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality” for each criterion.*** If the reviewer finds that the OER under consideration demonstrates the majority of performance indicators described in the middle column—*Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality*—it is recommended that the reviewer assign that category for that row of the rubric. The reviewer may choose to provide specific feedback on any performance indicator(s) in the middle column that either does not meet or exceeds the standards but is not required to do so. 

***Recommended threshold for deciding to use a specific OER with learners.*** This rubric is intended to be one resource faculty can use in the development/creation and/or selection of OER for use with their learners in the college or university classroom. It is not intended to serve as a gatekeeper to OER adoption, nor should it be used to be the sole, summative evaluation tool for OER adoption. Likewise, it is not a tool designed to assign an overall “grade” or total score to OER. Rather, each row of the rubric is meant to provide useful and ultimately actionable feedback vis-à-vis an attribute of OER that is reviewed with this tool. Other factors not evaluated by this or any other Open4Review rubric may lead a faculty member to decide to use a specific OER. As such, there is no single, established threshold for scoring with this rubric that should determine whether an OER should be adopted. Rather, evaluation of OER with this rubric, in conjunction with faculty exercising their expert judgment, can help identify relative strengths and weaknesses of OER in relation to the faculty’s intended use, classroom context, pedagogical approach, and learners’ needs. The decision to use a specific OER or not is ultimately a decision of faculty themselves.

***Best practices for using the rubric.*** Reviewers should evaluate each criterion independently, providing specific feedback in the appropriate columns. The "Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality" column represents the minimum acceptable standard for disciplinary appropriateness in OER textbooks. When using this rubric, evaluators should:

* **Test the eLearning **by accessing and using them as both an instructor and a student would, across multiple devices and platforms where possible.  
* **Provide specific, actionable feedback **in the "Does Not Meet Established Standards of Quality" column when standards are not achieved, including examples of functional or usability issues.  
* **Note exemplary practices **in the "Exceeds Established Standards of Quality" column, such as particularly innovative or effective eLearning implementations.  
* **Consider the OER's intended audience **and learning objectives when evaluating eLearning appropriateness.  
* **Provide evidence-based justification **for ratings through specific examples from eLearnibg usage.  
* **Consult the glossary **for clarification of technical terms.  
* **Focus on eLearning quality and effectiveness** rather than personal preferences for specific technologies.  
* **Evaluate eLearning in context **of how they support the specific learning objectives of the OER.

Multiple evaluators should review the same textbook independently to ensure reliability, with opportunities for discussion and calibration of ratings. Not all criteria will be applicable to every OER; evaluators may leave rows for criteria that are not applicable scoreless when an OER does not incorporate eLearning tools or specific technological features.

**Glossary of Terms**

**Asynchronous Communication**: Interaction that does not require participants to be engaged simultaneously, allowing for flexible participation timing (e.g., discussion forums, email).

**Cognitive Load**: The amount of mental processing capacity required to use a tool or engage with learning material; effective tools minimize extraneous cognitive load.

**Cross-Platform Compatibility**: The ability of a tool to function consistently across different operating systems, devices, and browsers.

**Data Privacy**: Protections and protocols ensuring that user information is collected, stored, and used appropriately and securely.

**Hypermediality**: The capacity to communicate through multiple channels (audio, visual, textual) in non-sequential, flexible ways that adapt to user needs.

**Interoperability**: The ability of a tool to integrate and work seamlessly with other systems, particularly Learning Management Systems (LMS).

**Learning Analytics**: Data collected about learner performance, engagement, and progress that can inform instructional decisions.

**Learning Management System (LMS):** A software application for the administration, documentation, tracking, reporting, and delivery of educational courses.

**Mobile Responsiveness**: Design that adapts to smaller screens and touch interfaces, maintaining full functionality on mobile devices.

**Offline Functionality**: The capacity to access and use tool features without an internet connection.

**Pedagogical Alignment**: The degree to which a tool's features and functions support intended learning objectives and instructional strategies.

**Scalability**: The ability of a tool to accommodate varying numbers of users, from small classes to large enrollments, without degradation of performance.

**Social Presence**: The degree to which participants feel connected to and aware of others in an online learning environment.

**Synchronous Communication**: Real-time interaction requiring participants to be engaged simultaneously (e.g., video conferencing, live chat).

**Technical Support**: Assistance available to users for troubleshooting, learning tool features, and resolving technical problems.

**Usability:** The ease with which users can learn to operate and effectively use a tool to accomplish intended tasks.

**User Interface (UI)**: The visual and interactive elements through which users engage with a digital tool.

**User Experience (UX)**: The overall experience and satisfaction a person has when using a tool, including ease of use, efficiency, and pleasure in interaction.

Mobile design appropriately addresses constraints of smaller screens while maintaining core functionality. There is minimal functional difference between mobile and desktop versions. eLearning functions effectively across both iOS and Android operating systems. Users can access essential features regardless of the device, operating system, or browser they choose. |  |
| **Criteria 4: Learning Management System (LMS) Integration  and Interoperability**   |  | eLearning can be embedded or fully integrated into LMS while maintaining full functionality. Integration is achieved through standard protocols (LTI compliance) that preserve user experience. Grade data, if applicable, passes back to the LMS gradebook seamlessly. Single sign-on authentication works properly. The tool does not require students to create separate external accounts when accessed through the LMS. |  |
| **Criteria 5: Data Privacy, Security, and Ownership**  |  | eLearning adheres to data privacy regulations and institutional policies (FERPA, GDPR as applicable). Privacy policies are clearly stated and easily accessible. Users maintain ownership of their intellectual property and data. If external accounts are required, eLearning has been vetted for data protection compliance. Users can save, archive, export, and download their work in multiple standard formats. Content is not locked into proprietary systems. Student data is not shared with third parties for commercial purposes. |  |
| **Criteria 6: Cost, Sustainability, and Resource Requirements**  |  | All essential features of eLearning are available free of charge for educational use, or costs are clearly disclosed upfront and are reasonable. If fees are required, they are unlikely to create financial barriers for students. eLearning is supported by a stable organization with evidence of long-term sustainability. Proper use does not require specialized equipment beyond what is typically available to students (computer, internet connection, standard peripherals). There are no hidden costs or required upgrades that limit functionality. |  |
| **Criteria 7: Data Privacy and Security**  |  | eLearning adheres to data privacy regulations and institutional policies (FERPA, GDPR as applicable). Privacy policies are clearly stated and easily accessible. Users maintain ownership of their intellectual property and data. eLearning provides appropriate controls for data privacy settings. If external accounts are required, eLearning has been vetted for data protection compliance. Student data is not shared with third parties for commercial purposes.  |  |
| **Criteria 8: Accessibility[^1]**  |  | eLearning meets accessibility guidelines (WCAG 2.1 AA standards, Section 508 requirements). The interface works effectively with assistive technologies including screen readers and keyboard navigation. Color contrast meets accessibility standards. Alternative text is provided for images. Videos include captions. eLearning is designed to address needs of diverse users with varying abilities and learning preferences. No learners are prevented from participating due to accessibility barriers. |  |
| **Criteria 9: Pedagogical Effectiveness and Learning Enhancement**  |  | eLearning demonstrably supports and enhances the intended learning objectives of the OER. Features align with sound pedagogical principles and effective instructional strategies. eLearning adds meaningful value beyond what could be achieved through simpler means. E Learning facilitates higher-order thinking skills appropriate to the content and level. eLearning actively engages students through interactive, meaningful activities. Students receive timely, actionable feedback on their performance. |  |
| **Criteria 10: Learning Analytics and Customization**  |  | eLearning collects meaningful data on student engagement, performance, and progress. Analytics are presented in a user-friendly format that instructors can easily interpret and use for instructional decision-making. Data can be accessed at both individual and aggregate levels. Instructors can easily customize eLearning to suit their specific course context, learning outcomes, and teaching approaches. eLearning can be adapted to different pedagogical strategies. Course management features are efficient and do not create excessive administrative burden. |  |
