  
**Open 4 Peer Review Project**  
**Copy Editing Rubric**

The *Open 4 Peer Review* Project aims to increase faculty adoption and adaptation of Open Educational Resources (OER) by developing and applying standards and rubrics to course materials for high-enrollment courses. A barrier to faculty use of OER is the lack of qualitative indicators that can support robust peer review. A barrier to faculty use of OER is the lack of qualitative indicators that can support robust peer review. A consortium of colleges and universities worked to address this barrier by (1) adapting existing, and creating new, openly licensed standards of quality for OER, (2) developing corresponding rubrics for applying those standards in a peer review process.

**Operational Definition for Copy Editing**

***Copy editing*** for Open Educational Resources (OER) refers to the professional standard of editorial review that ensures grammatical correctness, mechanical accuracy, stylistic consistency, and clear communication throughout educational materials. Copy editing encompasses the systematic correction of spelling, punctuation, capitalization, and syntax errors; the consistent application of style guide conventions; the verification of internal consistency in terminology, formatting, and usage; and the enhancement of readability and comprehension for the intended audience. High-quality copy editing in OER maintains the author's voice and disciplinary conventions while removing barriers to understanding, ensuring professional presentation, and supporting accessibility for diverse learners.

**Framing Language**

***Single-point rubric format.*** A single-point rubric differs from traditional holistic or analytic rubrics by focusing on a single column that describes the expected standard or proficient performance for each criterion. Rather than providing multiple performance levels with detailed descriptions, this format presents the target expectation in the center column, with space for feedback on areas needing improvement (left column) and areas exceeding expectations (right column). This design promotes clearer communication of standards while allowing for personalized, constructive feedback that guides improvement and recognizes excellence.

***Singularly focused rubric.*** Each of the rubrics in the Open 4 Peer Review suite of rubrics is designed to focus on one six areas for OER peer review: (1) Accessibility, (2) Copy Editing, (3) Copyright, (4) Disciplinary Appropriateness, (5) eLearning Review, and (6) Universal Design for Learning. **This rubric focuses exclusively on Copy Editing, and reviewers should therefore focus only on evaluating the OER on the criteria and the associated established standards of quality articulated on this rubric.** Review of the same OER with the other Open 4 Peer Review rubrics provide the most appropriate way to address issues/concerns unrelated to Copy Editing.

***Intended use.*** This rubric is designed to support faculty peer review of open educational resources to ensure standards of quality are met before adoption or publication. Specifically, this rubric helps reviewers evaluate whether OER materials demonstrate the quality of copy editing expected for professional educational publishing, providing faculty and institutions with confidence that materials are free from distracting errors, consistently formatted, and clearly written. This rubric can be used by peer reviewers, editors, project managers, or quality assurance teams as part of the OER development or evaluation process. Faculty/educators can edit the rubric's existing criteria and/or add additional criteria if they deem it necessary for their specific context or institutional requirements

***Nature of the data generated by the rubric.*** Data generated by this rubric are ***categorical*** in nature. Categorical data can be sorted into distinct groups or categories rather than measured on a continuous numerical scale. In rubric scoring, categorical data refers to performance levels (e.g., "Does Not Meet," "Meets," "Exceeds") that represent qualitatively different categories of achievement rather than precise numerical measurements. Each category describes a distinct level of quality, and scores represent classification into these discrete groups rather than points on a continuous scale. This approach emphasizes descriptive feedback and developmental progression rather than numerical ranking. 

***Threshold for achieving “Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality” for each criterion.*** If the reviewer finds that the OER under consideration demonstrates the majority of performance indicators described in the middle column—*Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality*—it is recommended that the reviewer assign that category for that row of the rubric. The reviewer may choose to provide specific feedback on any performance indicator(s) in the middle column that either does not meet or exceeds the standards but is not required to do so. 

***Recommended threshold for deciding to use a specific OER with learners.*** This rubric is intended to be one resource faculty can use in the development/creation and/or selection of OER for use with their learners in the college or university classroom. It is not intended to serve as a gatekeeper to OER adoption, nor should it be used to be the sole, summative evaluation tool for OER adoption. Likewise, it is not a tool designed to assign an overall “grade” or total score to OER. Rather, each row of the rubric is meant to provide useful and ultimately actionable feedback vis-à-vis an attribute of OER that is reviewed with this tool. Other factors not evaluated by this or any other Open4Review rubric may lead a faculty member to decide to use a specific OER. As such, there is no single, established threshold for scoring with this rubric that should determine whether an OER should be adopted. Rather, evaluation of OER with this rubric, in conjunction with faculty exercising their expert judgment, can help identify relative strengths and weaknesses of OER in relation to the faculty’s intended use, classroom context, pedagogical approach, and learners’ needs. The decision to use a specific OER or not is ultimately a decision of faculty themselves.

***Best practices for using the rubric.*** Reviewers should evaluate each criterion independently, providing specific feedback in the appropriate columns. The "Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality" column represents the minimum acceptable standard for copy editing of OER textbooks. When using this rubric, reviewers should:

* **Review the entire OER** or substantial representative sections before beginning evaluation  
* **Provide specific, actionable feedback** in the "Does Not Meet Established Standards of Quality" column when standards are not achieved  
* **Note exemplary practices** in the "Exceeds Established Standards of Quality" column  
* **Provide evidence-based justification** for ratings in comments or supporting documentation  
* **Consult the glossary** for clarification of technical terms  
* **Consult with copy editing specialists** when uncertain about technical requirements

Multiple evaluators should review the same textbook independently to ensure reliability, with opportunities for discussion and calibration of ratings.

**Glossary of Terms**

**Answer Keys:** Complete solutions or correct responses provided for exercises, assessments, or practice problems that enable verification of student work.

**Consistency:** The uniform application of editorial choices throughout a text, including spelling variants, terminology, formatting conventions, and stylistic preferences.

**Inclusive Language**: Word choices that avoid bias, stereotyping, or discrimination based on gender, race, ethnicity, disability, age, sexual orientation, or other characteristics.

**Internal Cross-References**: Links or citations within a text that direct readers to related content in other sections, chapters, or pages of the same resource.

**Mechanics:** The technical aspects of writing including punctuation, capitalization, spelling, abbreviations, and number usage.

**Parallelism**: The use of consistent grammatical structure across similar elements such as lists, headings, or series of phrases.

**Plain Language**: Clear, straightforward writing that the intended audience can understand on first reading, avoiding unnecessary jargon or complexity.

**Readability**: The ease with which a reader can understand written text, influenced by sentence structure, word choice, organization, and formatting.

**Technical Notation**: Specialized symbols, formulas, or formatting conventions specific to academic disciplines (e.g., mathematical equations, chemical formulas, statistical notation, programming code).

**Usage**: The conventional way words and phrases are employed in a language or discipline, including distinctions between commonly confused words and discipline-specific terminology.

**Copy Editing Rubric (Single-Point Rubric)**

|  | Exceeds Established Standards of Quality | Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality | Does Not Meet Established Standards of Quality |
| :---- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Criteria 1: Grammar and Syntax** |  | Text demonstrates proper sentence construction with correct subject-verb agreement, pronoun-antecedent agreement, and appropriate verb tenses. Sentences are complete and well-formed. Modifiers are correctly placed. Fragments and run-ons are intentional and effective where used. Sentence variety supports readability and maintains reader engagement. |  |
| **Criteria 2: Spelling and Typographical Accuracy** |  | All words are spelled correctly according to the chosen language variant (e.g., American or British English). Technical and discipline-specific terms are spelled accurately and consistently. Proper nouns are correctly spelled throughout. Typographical errors are absent or extremely rare (fewer than one per 5,000 words). |  |
| **Criteria 3: Punctuation and Mechanics** |  | Punctuation follows the conventions of the chosen style guide consistently. Commas, semicolons, colons, apostrophes, quotation marks, hyphens, and dashes are used correctly. Capitalization follows established conventions for titles, headings, proper nouns, and sentence beginnings. Number usage (numerals vs. words) follows style guide rules consistently. |  |
| **Criteria 4: Style Guide Consistency** |  | A recognized editorial style guide (e.g., APA, Chicago, MLA, institutional style) is applied consistently throughout the text. Abbreviations, acronyms, terminology, and formatting choices are uniform. Deviations from the style guide are intentional and appropriate for the discipline or context. |  |
| **Criteria 5: Internal Consistency and Usage** |  | Terminology is used consistently throughout (e.g., "learner" vs. "student," "e-learning" vs. "eLearning"). Commonly confused words are used correctly (e.g., affect/effect, complement/compliment). Discipline-specific usage conventions are followed. Parallel structure is maintained in lists, headings, and series. Point of view and voice are consistent unless intentionally varied for pedagogical purposes. |  |
| **Criteria 6: Clarity and Readability**  |  | Sentences are clear, concise, and accessible to the intended audience. Plain language is used appropriately; jargon is defined when first introduced. Sentence length and complexity are appropriate for the target audience. Transitions between ideas are smooth and logical. Ambiguity is eliminated. Text flows naturally and maintains reader comprehension. |  |
| **Criteria 7: Formatting and Visual Consistency** |  | Headings follow a logical hierarchical structure with consistent formatting. Font usage, size, and styling (bold, italic) are applied consistently and appropriately. Lists (bulleted and numbered) are formatted consistently. Spacing (line, paragraph, and section) is uniform throughout. Visual elements maintain professional presentation standards. |  |
| **Criteria 8:  Citations, References, and Attributions** |  | In-text citations follow the chosen citation style accurately and consistently. Reference lists or bibliographies are complete, accurately formatted, and properly ordered. All borrowed material is appropriately cited. Attribution statements for images, media, and adapted content are present, accurate, and properly formatted according to Creative Commons or licensing requirements. |  |
| **Criteria 9: Cross-References and Navigational Elements** |  | Internal cross-references (chapter, section, page, figure, and table references) are accurate and functional. Hyperlinks work correctly and open as intended. Figure and table numbers are sequential and correspond correctly to text references. Glossary terms, index entries, and other navigational aids are accurate and complete. | |  |
| **Criteria 10: Inclusive and Accessible Language** |  | Language is inclusive and avoids bias related to gender, race, ethnicity, disability, age, sexual orientation, and other characteristics. Cultural sensitivity is evident in examples and references. Language supports accessibility for diverse learners, including those using assistive technologies. Idioms and colloquialisms are used judiciously with consideration for multilingual audiences. |  |
