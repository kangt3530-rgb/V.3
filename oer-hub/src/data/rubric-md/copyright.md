  
**Open 4 Peer Review Project**  
**Copyright Review Rubric (Single-Point Rubric)**

The *Open 4 Peer Review* Project aims to increase faculty adoption and adaptation of Open Educational Resources (OER) by developing and applying standards and rubrics to course materials for high-enrollment courses. A barrier to faculty use of OER is the lack of qualitative indicators that can support robust peer review. A barrier to faculty use of OER is the lack of qualitative indicators that can support robust peer review. A consortium of colleges and universities worked to address this barrier by (1) adapting existing, and creating new, openly licensed standards of quality for OER, (2) developing corresponding rubrics for applying those standards in a peer review process.

**Operational Definition for Copyright Review**

***Copyright Review*** for Open Educational Resources (OER) or Open Educational Resources (OER) refers to the systematic examination of educational materials to ensure proper legal authorization for all included content, appropriate application of copyright exceptions (including fair use), accurate licensing documentation, and clear communication of reuse permissions. High-quality copyright practices in OER demonstrate proper attribution of sources, appropriate selection and application of open licenses, documented justification for fair use claims when applicable, compliance with third-party license requirements, and transparent communication about copyright status and usage rights for all components of the resource.

**Framing Language**

***Single-point rubric format.*** A single-point rubric differs from traditional holistic or analytic rubrics by focusing on a single column that describes the expected standard or proficient performance for each criterion. Rather than providing multiple performance levels with detailed descriptions, this format presents the target expectation in the center column, with space for feedback on areas needing improvement (left column) and areas exceeding expectations (right column). This design promotes clearer communication of standards while allowing for personalized, constructive feedback that guides improvement and recognizes excellence.

***Singularly focused rubric.*** Each of the rubrics in the Open 4 Peer Review suite of rubrics is designed to focus on one six areas for OER peer review: (1) Accessibility, (2) Copy Editing, (3) Copyright, (4) Disciplinary Appropriateness, (5) eLearning, and (6) Universal Design for Learning. **This rubric focuses exclusively on Copyright, and reviewers should therefore focus only on evaluating the OER on the criteria and the associated established standards of quality articulated on this rubric.** Review of the same OER with the other Open 4 Peer Review rubrics provide the most appropriate way to address issues/concerns unrelated to copyright.

***Intended use.*** This rubric is designed to support faculty peer review of open educational resources to ensure standards of quality are met before adoption or publication. Specifically, this rubric helps reviewers evaluate whether digital tools and technologies embedded within or accompanying OER materials demonstrate the functionality, pedagogical effectiveness, technical reliability, and accessibility expected for effective online and technology-enhanced learning. This rubric is discipline-

agnostic and can be applied to OER materials from any field that incorporate digital learning tools. Faculty/educators can edit the rubric's existing criteria and/or add additional criteria if they deem it necessary for their specific context.

***Nature of the data generated by the rubric.*** Data generated by this rubric are ***categorical*** in nature. Categorical data can be sorted into distinct groups or categories rather than measured on a continuous numerical scale. In rubric scoring, categorical data refers to performance levels (e.g., "Does Not Meet," "Meets," "Exceeds") that represent qualitatively different categories of achievement rather than precise numerical measurements. Each category describes a distinct level of quality, and scores represent classification into these discrete groups rather than points on a continuous scale. This approach emphasizes descriptive feedback and developmental progression rather than numerical ranking. 

***Threshold for achieving “Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality” for each criterion.*** If the reviewer finds that the OER under consideration demonstrates the majority of performance indicators described in the middle column—*Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality*—it is recommended that the reviewer assign that category for that row of the rubric. The reviewer may choose to provide specific feedback on any performance indicator(s) in the middle column that either does not meet or exceeds the standards but is not required to do so. 

***Recommended threshold for deciding to use a specific OER with learners.*** This rubric is intended to be one resource faculty can use in the development/creation and/or selection of OER for use with their learners in the college or university classroom. It is not intended to serve as a gatekeeper to OER adoption, nor should it be used to be the sole, summative evaluation tool for OER adoption. Likewise, it is not a tool designed to assign an overall “grade” or total score to OER. Rather, each row of the rubric is meant to provide useful and ultimately actionable feedback vis-à-vis an attribute of OER that is reviewed with this tool. Other factors not evaluated by this or any other Open4Review rubric may lead a faculty member to decide to use a specific OER. As such, there is no single, established threshold for scoring with this rubric that should determine whether an OER should be adopted. Rather, evaluation of OER with this rubric, in conjunction with faculty exercising their expert judgment, can help identify relative strengths and weaknesses of OER in relation to the faculty’s intended use, classroom context, pedagogical approach, and learners’ needs. The decision to use a specific OER or not is ultimately a decision of faculty themselves.

***Best practices for using the rubric.*** Reviewers should evaluate each criterion independently, providing specific feedback in the appropriate columns. The "Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality" column represents the minimum acceptable standard for disciplinary appropriateness in OER textbooks. When using this rubric, evaluators should:

* **Examine all content systematically **by reviewing text, images, videos, audio files, graphs, tables, and other incorporated materials for proper copyright documentation.  
* **Provide specific, actionable feedback **in the "Does Not Meet Established Standards of Quality" column when standards are not achieved, including identification of specific materials lacking proper documentation or attribution.  
* **Note exemplary practices **in the "Exceeds Established Standards of Quality" column, such as particularly thorough documentation, innovative fair use applications, or exceptionally clear rights statements.  
* **Consider the OER's intended audience **when evaluating whether copyright information is communicated clearly and appropriately.  
* **Provide evidence-based justification **for ratings through specific examples.  
* **Consult the glossary **for clarification of copyright and licensing terms.  
* **Focus on legal compliance and best practices** rather than personal preferences about licensing approaches.  
* **Evaluate copyright practices in context **of how they support the openness and adaptability of the OER.

Multiple evaluators should review the same textbook independently to ensure reliability, with opportunities for discussion and calibration of ratings. 

**Glossary of Terms**

**Attribution**: The practice of crediting original creators of content, typically including author/creator name, title of work, source, and license information where applicable.

**Creative Commons (CC) Attribution:**

* **CC BY**: A license allowing others to distribute, remix, adapt, and build upon a work, even commercially, as long as they credit the original creator.  
* **CC BY-NC (NonCommercial)**: A license allowing others to remix, adapt, and build upon a work non-commercially, with credit to the original creator.  
* **CC BY-SA (ShareAlike)**: A license allowing others to remix, adapt, and build upon a work, even commercially, as long as they credit the original creator and license new creations under identical terms.  
* **CC0 (Zero)**: A public domain dedication allowing creators to waive all copyright and related rights to a work.

**Copyright**: Legal protection granted to creators of original works, giving them exclusive rights to reproduce, distribute, display, perform, and create derivative works.

**Fair Use**: A legal doctrine (Section 107 of U.S. Copyright Act) permitting limited use of copyrighted material without permission for purposes such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, or research.

**Incompatible Licenses**: Licenses with terms that conflict with each other, making it legally impossible to combine materials under different licenses in the same work.

**License Compatibility**: The ability to legally combine materials under different open licenses within a single work without violating license terms.

**Open License**: A license that grants permission for others to use, adapt, and redistribute a work with minimal restrictions, typically while retaining attribution requirements.

**Orphan Work**: A copyrighted work whose copyright owner is difficult or impossible to identify or locate.

**Public Domain**: Works not protected by copyright, either because protection has expired, was forfeited, or never applied; these works can be used freely without permission.

**Transformative Use**: In fair use analysis, a use that adds new expression, meaning, or message to the original work, rather than merely superseding the original.

**Third-Party Content**: Material created by someone other than the OER author(s) that is incorporated into the resource.

**Work for Hire**: A work created by an employee within the scope of employment, where the employer owns the copyright, or a commissioned work under specific contractual terms.

| **Criteria 4: Fair Use Application and Documentation**  |  | When copyrighted materials are incorporated under fair use, the use aligns with established fair use principles (purpose and character, nature of work, amount used, market effect). Fair use is applied appropriately for purposes such as criticism, comment, illustration for teaching, or scholarship. The amount of copyrighted material used is appropriate and justified by the pedagogical purpose. Fair use reliance is documented or signaled appropriately, enabling downstream users to understand the copyright status of components. |  |
| **Criteria 5: License Compatibility and Documentation**  |  | All incorporated openly licensed materials are used in compliance with their respective license terms. When combining materials under different licenses, the licenses are compatible and the resulting work complies with all applicable license requirements. If using CC BY-SA or other share-alike materials, the OER is licensed compatibly to allow legal adaptation. No incompatible licenses are combined in ways that create legal conflicts. |  |
| **Criteria 6: Public Domain Materials**  |  | Materials identified as public domain are accurately categorized as such based on copyright expiration, government works status, CC0 dedication, or other appropriate justifications. Public domain status is clearly communicated to users. When public domain determination is complex (e.g., pre-1928 works, government works with exceptions), adequate documentation or explanation is provided. Public domain materials are distinguished from openly licensed materials. |  |
| **Criteria 7: Copyright Status Communication**  |  | Each component of the OER clearly communicates its copyright status and reuse permissions to adopters and adapters. A copyright statement or rights page explains the licensing approach used in the resource and provides guidance for users. The resource distinguishes between different types of content (original, openly licensed third-party, fair use, public domain) in a way that enables users to understand their rights and obligations. Information is presented clearly enough that educators without specialized copyright knowledge can understand and properly reuse the materials. |  |
| **Criteria 8: Permissions and Documentation Trail**  |  | For any materials used with explicit permission (beyond open licenses, public domain, or fair use), documentation of permission is maintained and can be provided upon request. Permission documentation includes scope of use granted, attribution requirements, and any restrictions. Contact information for permission-holders is retained. The resource includes clear indication of what permissions cover and any limitations on use. |  |
