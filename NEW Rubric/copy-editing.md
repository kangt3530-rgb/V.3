# Copy Editing

Copy editing is a key component of OER quality, ensuring that materials are clear, consistent, and free of errors that can distract or confuse learners. Well-edited resources support comprehension by presenting information in a polished and professional manner, allowing learners to focus on content rather than struggling with avoidable issues in grammar, spelling, or formatting.

In OER, copy editing is especially important because resources are often shared, adapted, and reused across contexts. Errors can be carried forward or amplified as materials are redistributed, potentially affecting credibility and usability. High-quality OER demonstrate attention to detail, consistency in style, and clarity in communication, which contributes to both learner understanding and faculty confidence in using the resource.

The criteria in this section outline standards that support clear, accurate, and consistent presentation of content. These standards are intended to guide constructive, improvement-focused feedback, helping reviewers identify areas where clarity and readability can be strengthened.

---

## C1: Grammar and Syntax

1. Text demonstrates proper sentence construction with correct subject-verb agreement, pronoun-antecedent agreement, and appropriate verb tenses.
2. Sentences are complete and well-formed.
3. Modifiers are correctly placed.
4. Fragments and run-ons are intentional and effective where used.
5. Sentence variety supports readability and maintains reader engagement.

## C2: Spelling and Typographical Accuracy

1. All words are spelled correctly according to the chosen language variant (e.g., American or British English).
2. Technical and discipline-specific terms are spelled accurately and consistently.
3. Proper nouns are correctly spelled throughout.
4. Typographical errors are absent or extremely rare (fewer than one per 5,000 words).

## C3: Punctuation and Mechanics

1. Punctuation follows the conventions of the chosen style guide consistently.
2. Commas, semicolons, colons, apostrophes, quotation marks, hyphens, and dashes are used correctly.
3. Capitalization follows established conventions for titles, headings, proper nouns, and sentence beginnings.
4. Number usage (numerals vs. words) follows style guide rules consistently.

## C4: Style Guide Consistency

1. A recognized editorial style guide (e.g., APA, Chicago, MLA, institutional style) is applied consistently throughout the text.
2. Abbreviations, acronyms, terminology, and formatting choices are uniform.
3. Deviations from the style guide are intentional and appropriate for the discipline or context.

## C5: Internal Consistency and Usage

1. Terminology is used consistently throughout (e.g., "learner" vs. "student," "e-learning" vs. "eLearning").
2. Commonly confused words are used correctly (e.g., affect/effect, complement/compliment).
3. Discipline-specific usage conventions are followed.
4. Parallel structure is maintained in lists, headings, and series.
5. Point of view and voice are consistent unless intentionally varied for pedagogical purposes.

## C6: Clarity and Readability

1. Sentences are clear, concise, and accessible to the intended audience.
2. Plain language is used appropriately; jargon is defined when first introduced.
3. Sentence length and complexity are appropriate for the target audience.
4. Transitions between ideas are smooth and logical. Ambiguity is eliminated.
5. Text flows naturally and maintains reader comprehension.

## C7: Formatting and Visual Consistency

1. Headings follow a logical hierarchical structure with consistent formatting.
2. Font usage, size, and styling (bold, italic) are applied consistently and appropriately.
3. Lists (bulleted and numbered) are formatted consistently. Spacing (line, paragraph, and section) is uniform throughout.
4. Visual elements maintain professional presentation standards.

## C8: Citations, References, and Attributions

1. In-text citations follow the chosen citation style accurately and consistently.
2. Reference lists or bibliographies are complete, accurately formatted, and properly ordered.
3. All borrowed material is appropriately cited.
4. Attribution statements for images, media, and adapted content are present, accurate, and properly formatted according to Creative Commons or licensing requirements.

## C9: Cross-References and Navigational Elements

1. Internal cross-references (chapter, section, page, figure, and table references) are accurate and functional.
2. Hyperlinks work correctly and open as intended. Figure and table numbers are sequential and correspond correctly to text references.
3. Glossary terms, index entries, and other navigational aids are accurate and complete.

## C10: Inclusive and Accessible Language

1. Language is inclusive and avoids bias related to gender, race, ethnicity, disability, age, sexual orientation, and other characteristics.
2. Cultural sensitivity is evident in examples and references.
3. Language supports accessibility for diverse learners, including those using assistive technologies.
4. Idioms and colloquialisms are used judiciously with consideration for multilingual audiences.
