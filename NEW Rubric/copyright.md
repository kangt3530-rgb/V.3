# Copyright and Licensing

Copyright and licensing are essential dimensions of OER quality, ensuring that materials can be legally used, adapted, and shared. Clear and accurate licensing allows educators and institutions to confidently engage with OER—whether adopting, revising, or redistributing content—without uncertainty about permissions or restrictions.

This area is particularly important in OER because openness is defined not only by cost, but by the ability to retain, reuse, revise, remix, and redistribute content. When licensing is unclear, incorrect, or inconsistently applied, it can limit these permissions and reduce the usefulness and impact of a resource. High-quality OER make licensing transparent and easy to understand, enabling others to build upon the work while respecting the rights of creators.

In practice, this includes clearly identifying licenses, providing accurate attributions, and ensuring that all included materials are compatible with the stated license. Attention to copyright and licensing supports both legal compliance and the ethical use of open content, strengthening trust in OER as a reliable and reusable resource.

The criteria in this section outline key standards that support clear, consistent, and appropriate use of copyright and licensing in OER. These standards are intended to guide constructive, improvement-focused feedback, helping reviewers identify strengths and opportunities to enhance clarity, usability, and openness.

---

## C1: Original Content Licensing

1. All originally created content in the OER is clearly licensed under an appropriate open license (e.g., CC BY, CC BY-SA, CC BY-NC).
2. The license is prominently displayed and easily located by users.
3. The chosen license is appropriate for the intended use and adaptability of the resource.
4. License terms are applied consistently throughout the resource.

## C2: Third Party Content Documentation

1. All third-party content (images, graphs, tables, videos, audio, text excerpts) incorporated into the OER is clearly identified and documented.
2. For each third-party element, the source is noted with sufficient detail to locate the original.
3. The legal basis for inclusion (open license, public domain, fair use, or permission) is clearly indicated.
4. Documentation is provided in a consistent format throughout the resource.

## C3: Attribution Practices

1. Proper attribution is provided for all third-party content that requires it, following the applicable license requirements or citation standards.
2. Attribution includes, as appropriate and available: creator/author name, title of work, source/URL, license type, and indication of any modifications made.
3. Attribution is placed appropriately (near the content, in captions, or in a credits section) and is sufficiently detailed to allow users to verify sources.
4. Public domain and fair use materials include appropriate citations even when not legally required.

## C4: Fair Use Application and Documentation

1. When copyrighted materials are incorporated under fair use, the use aligns with established fair use principles (purpose and character, nature of work, amount used, market effect).
2. Fair use is applied appropriately for purposes such as criticism, comment, illustration for teaching, or scholarship.
3. The amount of copyrighted material used is appropriate and justified by the pedagogical purpose.
4. Fair use reliance is documented or signaled appropriately, enabling downstream users to understand the copyright status of components.

## C5: License Compatibility and Documentation

1. All incorporated openly licensed materials are used in compliance with their respective license terms.
2. When combining materials under different licenses, the licenses are compatible and the resulting work complies with all applicable license requirements.
3. If using CC BY-SA or other share-alike materials, the OER is licensed compatibly to allow legal adaptation.
4. No incompatible licenses are combined in ways that create legal conflicts.

## C6: Public Domain Materials

1. Materials identified as public domain are accurately categorized as such based on copyright expiration, government works status, CC0 dedication, or other appropriate justifications.
2. Public domain status is clearly communicated to users.
3. When public domain determination is complex (e.g., pre-1928 works, government works with exceptions), adequate documentation or explanation is provided.
4. Public domain materials are distinguished from openly licensed materials.

## C7: Copyright Status Communication

1. Each component of the OER clearly communicates its copyright status and reuse permissions to adopters and adapters.
2. A copyright statement or rights page explains the licensing approach used in the resource and provides guidance for users.
3. The resource distinguishes between different types of content (original, openly licensed third-party, fair use, public domain) in a way that enables users to understand their rights and obligations.
4. Information is presented clearly enough that educators without specialized copyright knowledge can understand and properly reuse the materials.

## C8: Permissions and Documentation Trail

1. For any materials used with explicit permission (beyond open licenses, public domain, or fair use), documentation of permission is maintained and can be provided upon request.
2. Permission documentation includes scope of use granted, attribution requirements, and any restrictions.
3. Contact information for permission-holders is retained.
4. The resource includes clear indication of what permissions cover and any limitations on use.
