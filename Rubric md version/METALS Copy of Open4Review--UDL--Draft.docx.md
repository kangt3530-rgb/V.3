  
**Open 4 Peer Review Project**  
**Universal Design for Learning (UDL) Rubric**

The *Open 4 Peer Review* Project aims to increase faculty adoption and adaptation of Open Educational Resources (OER) by developing and applying standards and rubrics to course materials for high-enrollment courses. A barrier to faculty use of OER is the lack of qualitative indicators that can support robust peer review. A consortium of colleges and universities worked to address this barrier by (1) adapting existing, and creating new, openly licensed standards of quality for OER, (2) developing corresponding rubrics for applying those standards in a peer review process.

**Operational Definition for Universal Design for Learning (UDL)**

***Universal Design for Learning (UDL)*** for Open Educational Resources (OER) refers to a research-based educational framework that guides the proactive design of flexible learning environments and instructional materials to accommodate the full range of learner variability. Unlike accessibility, which focuses on technical compliance to remove barriers for users with disabilities (e.g., WCAG 2.1 standards for perceivability and operability of digital content), UDL is a pedagogical approach focused on instructional design choices that provide multiple pathways for engagement, representation, and action/expression.

UDL is grounded in three core principles derived from neuroscience and learning sciences: (1) **Multiple Means of Engagement** \- providing varied ways to motivate learners, recruit their interest, sustain effort and persistence, and support self-regulation; (2) **Multiple Means of Representation** \- presenting information and content in different formats, modalities, and levels of complexity; and (3) **Multiple Means of Action and Expression** \- allowing learners to demonstrate understanding through varied tools, media, and communication formats (CAST UDL Guidelines, 2024).

The goal of UDL is to support the “development of learner agency that is purposeful and reflective, resourceful and authentic, and strategic and action-oriented” (CAST UDL Guidelines 3.0, 2024). UDL recognizes that instructional flexibility built into materials from the outset reduces the need for individual accommodations and supports all learners in developing metacognitive capacity and self-directed learning skills (Peter & Baribeau, 2020). Importantly, UDL aims for **collective access** (Weirick Johnson & Abumeeiz, 2023)—designing materials that anticipate and address diverse learning needs proactively for the community of learners—rather than requiring learners to request individual accommodations after encountering barriers.

**Framing Language**

***Single-point rubric format.*** A single-point rubric differs from traditional holistic or analytic rubrics by focusing on a single column that describes the expected standard or proficient performance for each criterion. Rather than providing multiple performance levels with detailed descriptions, this format presents the target expectation in the center column, with space for feedback on areas needing improvement (left column) and areas exceeding expectations (right column). This design promotes clearer communication of standards while allowing for personalized, constructive feedback that guides improvement and recognizes excellence.

***Singularly focused rubric.*** Each of the rubrics in the Open 4 Peer Review suite of rubrics is designed to focus on one six areas for OER peer review: (1) Accessibility, (2) Copy Editing, (3) Copyright, (4) Disciplinary Appropriateness, (5) eLearning Review, and (6) Universal Design for Learning. **This rubric focuses exclusively on UDL, and reviewers should therefore focus only on evaluating the OER on the criteria and the associated established standards of quality articulated on this rubric.** Review of the same OER with the other Open 4 Peer Review rubrics provide the most appropriate way to address issues/concerns unrelated to UDL.

***Distinguishing UDL from Accessibility*****.** While UDL and accessibility are related and mutually reinforcing concepts, they address different aspects of educational quality and should not be conflated. Accessibility (evaluated in the separate Open4Review Accessibility rubric) focuses on technical compliance with standards such as WCAG 2.1 AA to ensure that content can be perceived, operated, understood, and navigated by users with disabilities—including those using assistive technologies such as screen readers, alternative keyboards, or voice recognition software. Accessibility addresses whether a learner can access the content technically. **UDL** (evaluated in this rubric) focuses on pedagogical and instructional design choices that provide flexibility, options, and multiple pathways for how learners engage with content, process and understand information, and demonstrate their knowledge. UDL addresses whether materials support diverse learning preferences, cultural frameworks, cognitive approaches, and levels of prior knowledge. UDL is about learner agency, choice, metacognitive development, and reducing barriers to learning—not just barriers to access. **As researchers Weirick Johnson and Abumeeiz (2023) caution, "accessibility is not problem solving" and should not be treated as a checklist to be completed after materials are created. **Instead, both accessibility and UDL should be foundational to the design process from the beginning. However, they serve different purposes: accessibility ensures everyone can access the content, while UDL ensures everyone has flexible pathways to learn from and engage with that content. Both are essential; neither is sufficient alone. This rubric evaluates UDL specifically, with the understanding that technical accessibility (evaluated separately) is a prerequisite baseline.

***Intended use.*** This rubric is designed to support faculty peer review of open educational resources to ensure standards of quality are met before adoption or publication.  Specifically, this rubric is designed to help educators evaluate whether selected OER incorporate UDL principles that support diverse learners through instructional flexibility, learner choice, and development of metacognitive skills. Faculty/educators can edit the rubric's existing criteria and/or add additional criteria if they deem it necessary. 

***Nature of the data generated by the rubric.*** Data generated by this rubric are ***categorical*** in nature. Categorical data can be sorted into distinct groups or categories rather than measured on a continuous numerical scale. In rubric scoring, categorical data refers to performance levels (e.g., "Does Not Meet," "Meets," "Exceeds") that represent qualitatively different categories of achievement rather than precise numerical measurements. Each category describes a distinct level of quality, and scores represent classification into these discrete groups rather than points on a continuous scale. This approach emphasizes descriptive feedback and developmental progression rather than numerical ranking. 

***Threshold for achieving “Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality” for each criterion.*** If the reviewer finds that the OER under consideration demonstrates the majority of performance indicators described in the middle column—*Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality*—it is recommended that the reviewer assign that category for that row of the rubric. The reviewer may choose to provide specific feedback on any performance indicator(s) in the middle column that either does not meet or exceeds the standards but is not required to do so. 

***Recommended threshold for deciding to use a specific OER with learners.*** This rubric is intended to be one resource faculty can use in the development/creation and/or selection of OER for use with their learners in the college or university classroom. It is not intended to serve as a gatekeeper to OER adoption, nor should it be used to be the sole, summative evaluation tool for OER adoption. Likewise, it is not a tool designed to assign an overall “grade” or total score to OER. Rather, each row of the rubric is meant to provide useful and ultimately actionable feedback vis-à-vis an attribute of OER that is reviewed with this tool. Other factors not evaluated by this or any other Open4Review rubric may lead a faculty member to decide to use a specific OER. As such, there is no single, established threshold for scoring with this rubric that should determine whether an OER should be adopted. Rather, evaluation of OER with this rubric, in conjunction with faculty exercising their expert judgment, can help identify relative strengths and weaknesses of OER in relation to the faculty’s intended use, classroom context, pedagogical approach, and learners’ needs. The decision to use a specific OER or not is ultimately a decision of faculty themselves.

***Best practices for using the rubric.*** Reviewers should evaluate each criterion independently, providing specific feedback in the appropriate columns. The "Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality" column represents the minimum acceptable standard for disciplinary appropriateness in OER textbooks. When using this rubric, evaluators should:

* **Review the entire OER** or substantial representative sections before beginning evaluation  
* **Focus on instructional design choices and pedagogical flexibility** rather than technical accessibility compliance (which is evaluated in the separate Accessibility rubric)  
* **Provide specific, actionable feedback** in the "Does Not Meet Established Standards of Quality" column when standards are not achieved.   
* **Note exemplary practices** in the "Exceeds Established Standards of Quality" column.   
* **Consider collective access** by evaluating whether materials proactively address diverse learning needs for the community rather than requiring individual learners to request accommodations.  
* **Evaluate whether learners have meaningful choices** rather than whether a single pathway is well-designed.  
* **Consider the OER's intended audience** and course level when evaluating appropriateness of options and flexibility.  
* **Recognize that UDL is iterative** - materials can always be improved, and ongoing refinement based on learner feedback is expected.  
* **Provide evidence-based justification** for ratings in comments or supporting documentation.  
* **Consult the glossary** for clarification of UDL-specific terms.  
* **Consider how the materials support learner agency** and development of self-directed learning skills.

Multiple evaluators should review the same textbook independently to ensure reliability, with opportunities for discussion and calibration of ratings.

**Glossary of Terms**  
**Authentic Assessment**: Tasks that mirror real-world applications, professional contexts, or meaningful problems rather than artificial or decontextualized exercises.  
**Bias**: Prejudice, assumptions, or favoritism—whether implicit or explicit—that may disadvantage certain groups of learners or privilege others based on race, ethnicity, language, socioeconomic status, ability, gender, sexual orientation, or other identity characteristics.  
**Cognitive Scaffolding**: Temporary instructional supports that help learners access complex content or complete challenging tasks, designed to be gradually removed as learner competence increases.  
**Construct Validity**: The degree to which an assessment measures what it intends to measure; in UDL contexts, ensuring that assessment format does not introduce irrelevant barriers to demonstrating knowledge or skills.  
**Executive Functions**: Higher-order cognitive processes including planning, organizing, strategizing, paying attention to details, managing time and space, and monitoring one's own actions and progress.  
**Mastery-Oriented Feedback**: Feedback that emphasizes progress toward learning goals, improvement over time, and effective strategies rather than comparing learners to peers or focusing on deficits.  
**Metacognition**: Awareness and understanding of one's own thought processes, including the ability to plan, monitor, evaluate, and regulate one's learning and problem-solving approaches.  
**Non-Linguistic Representation**: Visual, spatial, auditory, tactile, or kinesthetic ways of representing information that complement or provide alternatives to text (diagrams, models, animations, manipulatives, sound).  
**Open Licensing**: Legal permissions granted through licenses such as Creative Commons that allow users to retain, reuse, revise, remix, and redistribute educational resources, enabling adaptation to meet diverse learner needs.  
**Self-Regulation**: The ability to monitor and manage one's emotions, attention, behavior, and motivation to achieve learning goals, supported through UDL by providing tools and strategies for goal setting, progress monitoring, and reflection.  
**Stereotyping**: The use of oversimplified, generalized, or fixed ideas about groups of people that do not reflect the diversity and complexity within those groups and can perpetuate harmful biases.  
**Transfer**: The application of knowledge or skills learned in one context to new or different situations; UDL supports transfer through varied contexts, authentic applications, and explicit attention to underlying patterns and principles.

| **Criteria 1: Multiple Options for Representation and Perception** |  | Content is presented through multiple formats and modalities (text, visual, audio, video, interactive). Information is available in more than one sensory mode. Materials offer options for customizing display where appropriate (adjustable text size, layout options). | **Criteria 3: Engagement, Relevance, and Cultural Responsiveness** |  | Content explicitly connects to real-world applications, authentic problems, or professional contexts. Materials provide opportunities for learners to make meaningful choices related to topics, approaches, or applications that connect to their interests and goals. Visual and textual content represents diverse identities, perspectives, and cultural frameworks authentically, avoiding stereotypical or token representations. Examples and scenarios reflect varied lived experiences and cultural contexts. Materials acknowledge and address potential biases in language use, representation, and assumptions about learners' backgrounds. Activities incorporate opportunities for exploration, creativity, and/or joyful engagement where appropriate. |  |
| **Criteria 4: Framework Transparent Expectations, Models, and Actionable Feedback** |  | Learning objectives are explicitly stated in learner-accessible language that clarifies both goals and their value. Success criteria or rubrics are provided in advance with clear, specific descriptors (not vague terms). Models, exemplars, or non-models at varying levels are provided to illustrate expectations. Opportunities for formative feedback emphasize process, strategies, and improvement rather than relative performance or deficit-based critique. Scaffolds and supports are designed for gradual release, supporting increasing learner independence over time. |  |
| **Criteria 5: Collaboration, Communication, and Community** |  | Materials provide options for both individual and collaborative work. Collaborative activities include clear structures (defined roles, interdependence, individual accountability) that support equitable participation. Multiple communication formats are supported (synchronous/asynchronous, written/oral/visual). Activities create opportunities for learners to build community, contribute diverse perspectives, develop sense of belonging, and foster care and connection. Where appropriate, materials invite family or community participation to extend learning beyond the classroom. |  |
| **Criteria 6: Metacognition, Self-Regulation, and Strategic Learning** |  | Materials support learners in setting personal goals, monitoring progress, and reflecting on learning processes and outcomes. Opportunities for self-assessment and/or peer assessment are integrated with clear criteria. Prompts, tools, or activities help learners develop metacognitive awareness (planning, strategy selection, evaluation of understanding, recognition of learning preferences). Materials provide explicit support for organizing information and resources (templates, organizational tools, guides for managing tasks). Activities help learners break large goals into manageable steps, anticipate challenges, and develop planning strategies. Materials make thinking visible through modeling, worked examples, or prompts for learners to articulate their approaches. Materials encourage learner agency in making choices about learning approaches and advocating for needed supports. |  |
| **Criteria 7: Equity, Bias Awareness, and Inclusive Learning Environments** |  | Materials explicitly work to create learning environments that value and support all learners. Content and activities address potential sources of bias, stereotype threat, or exclusion. Strategies are included to reduce anxiety and increase access (predictable structures, options for managing sensory/social demands, flexible routines). Materials foster discussions about diverse perspectives and provide opportunities for learners to challenge exclusionary practices and analyze how diversity improves learning. Where appropriate, restorative or community-building approaches are integrated. Content challenges singular narratives and makes space for diverse interpretations and approaches. |  |
| **Criteria 8: OER-Specific UDL Considerations** |  | Materials leverage open licensing to enable adaptation and customization for diverse learner needs. Resources are available in multiple accessible file formats (not just formats that require proprietary software), supporting learners' varied technology access and preferences. If materials include multimedia, raw or editable files are provided to support remix and adaptation. Metadata or accessibility statements clearly describe UDL features, format options, and any customization possibilities, helping instructors and learners understand how materials can be adapted. Materials are designed with awareness that OER users may have limited technology access, providing low-bandwidth alternatives or downloadable options where appropriate. Open licensing is explicitly framed as supporting collective access by enabling community adaptation and improvement. |  |
