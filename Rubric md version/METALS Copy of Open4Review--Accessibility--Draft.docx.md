

**Open 4 Peer Review Project**  
**Accessibility Rubric (Single-Point Rubric)**

The *Open 4 Peer Review* Project aims to increase faculty adoption and adaptation of Open Educational Resources (OER) by developing and applying standards and rubrics to course materials for high-enrollment courses. A barrier to faculty use of OER is the lack of qualitative indicators that can support robust peer review. A consortium of colleges and universities worked to address this barrier by (1) adapting existing, and creating new, openly licensed standards of quality for OER, (2) developing corresponding rubrics for applying those standards in a peer review process.

**Operational Definition for Accessibility**

***Accessibility*** for Open Educational Resources (OER) refers to the design and development of educational materials that can be used by all learners, regardless of their abilities, disabilities, or technological constraints. Accessible OER ensures that content is perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust for users with diverse needs, including those who rely on assistive technologies.

**Framing Language**

***Single-point rubric format.*** A single-point rubric differs from traditional holistic or analytic rubrics by focusing on a single column that describes the expected standard or proficient performance for each criterion. Rather than providing multiple performance levels with detailed descriptions, this format presents the target expectation in the center column, with space for feedback on areas needing improvement (left column) and areas exceeding expectations (right column). This design promotes clearer communication of standards while allowing for personalized, constructive feedback that guides improvement and recognizes excellence.

***Singularly focused rubric.*** Each of the rubrics in the Open 4 Peer Review suite of rubrics is designed to focus on one of six areas for OER peer review: (1) Accessibility, (2) Copy Editing, (3) Copyright, (4) Disciplinary Appropriateness, (5) eLearning Review, and (6) Universal Design for Learning. **This rubric focuses exclusively on Accessibility, and reviewers should therefore focus only on evaluating the OER on the criteria and the associated established standards of quality articulated on this rubric.** Review of the same OER with the other Open 4 Peer Review rubrics provide the most appropriate way to address issues/concerns unrelated to Accessibility.

***Accessibility as a foundational design parameter.*** Researchers Weirick Johnson and Abumeeiz (2023) caution, "accessibility is not problem solving" and should not be treated as a checklist to be completed after materials are created.** **Instead, accessibility should be foundational to the design process from the beginning, and responsive and iterative should additional accessibility issues or concerns emerge. 

***Intended use.*** This rubric is designed to support faculty peer review of open educational resources to ensure standards of quality are met before adoption or publication. Specifically, this rubric is designed to help educators ensure that selected OER meet the accessibility standards expected within higher education contexts across all disciplines, providing faculty with confidence in assigning materials that support student learning at the appropriate academic level. Faculty/educators can edit the rubric’s existing criteria and/or add additional criteria if they deem it necessary. 

***Nature of the data generated by the rubric.*** Data generated by this rubric are ***categorical*** in nature. Categorical data can be sorted into distinct groups or categories rather than measured on a continuous numerical scale. In rubric scoring, categorical data refers to performance levels (e.g., "Does Not Meet," "Meets," "Exceeds") that represent qualitatively different categories of achievement rather than precise numerical measurements. Each category describes a distinct level of quality, and scores represent classification into these discrete groups rather than points on a continuous scale. This approach emphasizes descriptive feedback and developmental progression rather than numerical ranking. 

***Threshold for achieving “Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality” for each criterion.*** If the reviewer finds that the OER under consideration demonstrates the majority of performance indicators described in the middle column—*Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality*—it is recommended that the reviewer assign that category for that row of the rubric. The reviewer may choose to provide specific feedback on any performance indicator(s) in the middle column that either does not meet or exceeds the standards but is not required to do so. 

***Recommended threshold for deciding to use a specific OER with learners.*** This rubric is intended to be one resource faculty can use in the development/creation and/or selection of OER for use with their learners in the college or university classroom. It is not intended to serve as a gatekeeper to OER adoption, nor should it be used to be the sole, summative evaluation tool for OER adoption. Likewise, it is not a tool designed to assign an overall “grade” or total score to OER. Rather, each row of the rubric is meant to provide useful and ultimately actionable feedback vis-à-vis an attribute of OER that is reviewed with this tool. Other factors not evaluated by this or any other Open4Review rubric may lead a faculty member to decide to use a specific OER. As such, there is no single, established threshold for scoring with this rubric that should determine whether an OER should be adopted. Rather, evaluation of OER with this rubric, in conjunction with faculty exercising their expert judgment, can help identify relative strengths and weaknesses of OER in relation to the faculty’s intended use, classroom context, pedagogical approach, and learners’ needs. The decision to use a specific OER or not is ultimately a decision of faculty themselves.

***Best practices for using the rubric.*** Reviewers should evaluate each criterion independently, providing specific feedback in the appropriate columns. The "Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality" column represents the minimum acceptable standard for disciplinary appropriateness in OER textbooks. When using this rubric, evaluators should:

* **Review the entire OER** or substantial representative sections before beginning evaluation  
* **Provide specific, actionable feedback** in the "Does Not Meet Established Standards of Quality" column when standards are not achieved  
* **Note exemplary practices** in the "Exceeds Established Standards of Quality" column  
* **Consider the OER's intended audience** and course level when evaluating appropriateness  
* **Provide evidence-based justification** for ratings in comments or supporting documentation  
* **Consult the glossary** for clarification of technical terms  
* **Consult with accessibility specialists** when uncertain about technical requirements

Multiple evaluators should review the same textbook independently to ensure reliability, with opportunities for discussion and calibration of ratings.

**Glossary of Terms**

**Alternative Text (Alt Text)**: Text descriptions of images that are read by screen readers to convey the content and function of images to users who cannot see them.

**Assistive Technology**: Hardware or software designed to help people with disabilities access and use digital content, including screen readers, voice recognition software, and alternative keyboards.

**Closed Captions**: Text that appears on screen to represent spoken dialogue and sound effects in video content, synchronized with the audio.

**Contrast Ratio**: The difference in luminance between text and background colors, measured on a scale where 21:1 represents maximum contrast (black text on white background).

**Focus Indicator**: A visual indication showing which interactive element currently has keyboard focus, typically displayed as an outline or highlight.

**Heading Hierarchy**: The organizational structure of content using heading levels (H1 through H6) to create a logical outline that assistive technology users can navigate.

**Keyboard Accessible**: Interactive elements that can be reached and activated using only keyboard navigation, without requiring a mouse or touch input.

**Logical Nesting:**  The hierarchical organization of heading levels (H1-H6) in which each heading level follows a sequential order without skipping levels, creating a clear parent-child relationship between sections and subsections.

**Null Alt Text**: Empty alternative text (alt="") used for decorative images that convey no meaningful information, signaling assistive technology to skip the image.

**Programmatically Associated**: Content that is connected through code so that assistive technology can understand relationships between elements, such as labels and form fields.

**Scope Attributes**: HTML markup that explicitly defines whether table headers apply to rows, columns, or groups of cells.

**Screen Reader**: Assistive technology software that reads digital text aloud and provides navigation tools for users who are blind or have low vision.

**WCAG 2.1 AA**: Web Content Accessibility Guidelines Level AA, the internationally recognized standard for web accessibility compliance.

**Accessibility Rubric**

|  |  Exceeds Established Standards of Quality (3)  | Exemplifies Established Standards of Quality (2)  | Does Not Meet Established Standards of Quality (1)  |
| :---- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **Criteria 1: Text Structure and Screen Reader Navigation** |  | All text content includes proper heading hierarchy (H1-H6), with logical nesting and no skipped levels. Lists use proper markup (bulleted/numbered). Reading order matches visual layout and intended flow of information. Page navigation coding supports the use of adaptive technology. |  |
| **Criteria 2:  Visual Design and Color** |  | Text and background colors meet WCAG 2.1 AA contrast ratios (4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text). Information is not conveyed by color alone. Font size is minimum 12pt for body text. Visual layout supports logical reading flow.  |  |
| **Criteria 3: Alternative Text and Image Accessibility** |  | All informative images include descriptive alternative text. Decorative images are marked with null alt text. Charts and graphs include data tables or detailed descriptions.  |  |
| **Criteria 4: Multimedia Accessibility** |  | Videos include accurate closed captions and transcripts. Audio content provides complete transcripts. Media players are keyboard accessible and compatible with assistive technology. Auto-playing content can be paused or stopped. |  |
| **Criteria 5: Interactive Elements and Forms** |  | All interactive elements (links, buttons, form fields) are keyboard accessible. Form fields have labels and instructions. Error messages are descriptive and actionable to promote trouble-shooting. Focus indicators are visible and logical. |  |
| **Criteria 6: Table Structure and Data Presentation** |  | Data tables include proper header markup and scope attributes. Tables are not used for layout purposes. Complex tables provide summaries or navigation aids. Row and column relationships are clearly defined. |  |
| **Criteria 7: Link Quality and Context** |  | Link text is descriptive and meaningful out of context (in general, avoids "click here" or URL-only links unless links themselves are descriptive, e.g., http/smarthistory/michelangelos-david). Links indicate when opening in new windows or leading to different file formats. Link purposes are clear from context or link text alone.  |  |
| **Criteria 8: Technical Format and Compatibility** |  | Content is available in formats that preserve accessibility features. Resources work across multiple devices and platforms (responsive design). The learner does not need to use additional specific software or plugins to access resources. |  |
